# Summary 13 

The rapid expansion of biomedical literature demands automated summarization tools that reli-14 

ably condense research articles into concise, accurate summaries. We benchmarked 62 sum-15 

marization methods, ranging from frequency-based and TextRank extractors to encoder-decoder 16 

models (EDMs) and large language models (LLMs), on 1,000 biomedical abstracts from 20 jour-17 

nals across ScienceDirect and Cell Press , using author-written highlights as reference sum-18 

maries. Models were evaluated with a composite suite of lexical, semantic, and factual metrics, 19 

including ROUGE, BLEU, METEOR, embedding-based similarity, and factuality scores. General-20 

purpose models (e.g., Mistral, GPT, Llama) achieved the highest overall performance across lexi-21 

cal and semantic dimensions, outperforming reasoning-oriented (e.g., DeepSeek, Magistral) and 22 

domain-specific (e.g., BioGPT, BioMistral) models. Notably, medium-sized models outperformed 23 

large-scale models, suggesting an optimal balance between model capacity and efficiency, while 24 

classical extractive methods lagged behind neural approaches. These findings provide a system-25 

atic reference for selecting biomedical summarization tools and highlight that broad pretraining 26 

outperforms narrow domain adaptation. 27

# Introduction 31 

The exponential growth of scientific literature has created a demand for text summarization meth-32 

ods to support scientists in prioritizing articles, extracting relevant information, and interpreting 33 

research findings. Automatic text summarization (ATS) methods have evolved from statistical 34 

approaches to deep learning-based models, thus becoming increasingly sophisticated and reli-35 

able in capturing the essential parts of complex research articles. Although ATS methods have 36 

been previously evaluated and described 1,2 , only a few have focused on scientific literature 3,4 .37

Recent work has begun to benchmark large language models (LLMs) on biomedical summariza-38 

tion, though only for a limited set of models and typically against abstract-based references 5–7 .39 

A systematic comparison from word-frequency methods to state-of-the-art LLMs is still lacking. 40 

Over time, these methods have developed into distinct approaches that differ in how they 41 

identify or generate summary content. Early pre-neural summarization was mainly character-42 

ized by unsupervised extractive approaches that rank sentences by word or concept frequencies 43 

to identify relevant sentences. The field was initially shaped by Luhn’s assumption that recur-44 

rent words signal importance 8 and Edmundson’s use of cue words, title words, and sentence 45 

position 9. Later work adopting weighting term frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF) 46 

refined this idea by down-weighting frequent, low-specificity terms 10 , laying the foundation for 47 

more advanced methods in scientific text summarization 11 . Graph-based methods judged the 48 

importance against the document’s global structure rather than local word counts. TextRank, 49 

widely used in the biomedical domain, applies the PageRank algorithm to a sentence-similarity 50 

graph to select top-ranked sentences 12 .51 

The advent of sequence-to-sequence (Seq2seq) frameworks shifted the field toward neural 52 

approaches capable of paraphrasing and condensing text using encoder-decoder architectures, 53 

originally implemented with recurrent neural networks (RNNs), long short-term memory (LSTM) 54 

networks, and gated recurrent units (GRUs) 13,14 , while self-attention enabled parallel process-55 

ing of sequences and modeling of long-range context 15 . This laid the foundation for trans-56 

former architectures that quickly gained popularity in performing a wide range of natural lan-57 

guage processing (NLP) tasks. Building on bidirectional encoder representations from trans-58 

formers (BERT) 16 , several abstractive summarization models have emerged, including bidirec-59 

tional and auto-regressive transformer (BART), a denoising autoencoder for pretraining Seq2seq 60 

models 17 and its biomedical adaptations 18,19 ; the unified text-to-text transfer transformer (T5) 61 

model 20 ; Pre-training with extracted gap-sentences for abstractive summarization sequence-to-62 

sequence (PEGASUS) 21 , including domain-specific PubMed variants; and Longformer-based 63 

models 22 that can handle longer inputs 23 . These models are typically pretrained independently, 64 

while Longformer-based variants may additionally leverage RoBERTa, an optimized version of 65 

BERT trained on a larger corpus. 66 

More recently, the field of ATS has quickly moved toward decoder-only architectures at the 67 

core of LLMs, which capture semantic relations with greater flexibility and specificity. LLMs 68 

can be classified as (i) general-purpose models, which apply broad domain knowledge across 69 

diverse NLP tasks, such as the proprietary generative pre-trained transformer (GPT) 24 and 70 

Claude 25 series and the open-source large language model Meta AI (Llama) 26 and Gemma 27 

> 71

families; (ii) reasoning-oriented models, characterized by logical text understanding through it-72 

erative chain-of-thought processing and instruction tuning 28 such as DeepSeek-R1 29 , Qwen3 30 ,73 

and Mistral’s Magistral 31 , together with the reasoning tiers of the proprietary families (e.g., GPT-74 

5 and Claude Opus 4); and (iii) domain-specific models, tailored for specialized tasks or spe-75 

cific scientific domains , such as the biomedical models OpenBioLLM-Llama3 32 , BioGPT 33 , and 76 

BioMistral 34 , and SciLitLLM 35 for scientific literature. The complete set of models evaluated in 77 

this study is listed in Table 2 in the Materials and Methods. 78 

Despite this range of approaches, these method families have not been evaluated together on 79 

biomedical literature, leaving little guidance on which to choose. This study addresses this gap 80 

by systematically evaluating 62 text summarization models, ranging from word-frequency meth-81 

ods to state-of-the-art LLMs, using a dataset of 1,000 biomedical abstracts and corresponding 82 

highlights sections as reference summaries. We provide recommendations for selecting appro-83 

priate tools to accelerate knowledge discovery in biomedical sciences and discuss the strengths 84 

and limitations of each approach. 85

# Materials and Methods 86 

Study Design 87 

This study was designed as a systematic evaluation and comparative benchmark of ATS meth-88 

ods for biomedical literature and is reported in accordance with the transparent reporting of 89 

a multivariable prediction model for individual prognosis or diagnosis (TRIPOD)-LLM reporting 90 

guideline 36 . Summarization quality was assessed quantitatively using a suite of lexical, semantic, 91 

and factual metrics, normalized and aggregated into an Overall Performance Score for ranking, 92 

and complemented by a blinded expert assessment of a subset of models. Differences across 93 

model categories and families were then compared statistically. The evaluation metrics and 94 

statistical tests were chosen during the analysis rather than pre-specified in a study protocol. 95 

Figure 1 provides an overview of the complete benchmarking workflow of this study from 96 

dataset construction and model inference to metric computation, score aggregation, and statis-97 

tical analysis. 98 

Figure 1: Overview of the benchmarking workflow: (1) Construction of a gold-standard dataset comprising 1,000 biomedical titles, abstracts, and author-provided highlight sections. (2) Suite of 62 diverse summarization models. (3) Uniform prompting of all models to generate summaries for each abstract. (4–5) Computation of summary quality metrics capturing lexical overlap, semantic similarity, and factual consistency. (6) Standardization of metric scores using z-score normalization to ensure comparability across evaluation dimensions. (7) Ranking of models based on overall performance scores. (8) Quality assessment of generated summaries across four quality dimensions (coherence, fluency, relevance, consistency), combining expert human evaluation on a subset of models with (9) LLM-as-a-judge evaluation from three provider-independent judges run across all models.

Summarization Methods 120 

We evaluated 62 models for text summarization, ranging from simple frequency-based algo-121 

rithms to small language models (SLMs) and LLMs. These models were classified into five 122 

categories, as listed in Table 2. We obtained the pre-trained encoder-decoder models (EDMs) 123 

through the Hugging Face library, selecting architectures widely used for abstractive summa-124 

rization to ensure established neural approaches are covered in our benchmark. Additionally, 125 

we evaluated a range of SLMs and LLMs, defining SLMs as models with fewer than 10 billion 126 

parameters 39 . Both SLMs and LLMs with advanced reasoning capabilities were categorized as 127 

“reasoning-oriented” to investigate how multi-step problem solving affects summarization perfor-128 

mance. Similarly, models fine-tuned on scientific/biomedical data or specifically tailored for text 129 

summarization were classified as “domain-specific” to assess the impact of domain adaptation. 130 

Overall, this selection covers various model sizes and release dates, ensuring a representative 131 

mix of both, widely adopted and recent, architectures. 132 

Exceptionally large models, such as LLaMA 3.1 405B, were excluded to keep the benchmark 133 

reproducible on single-GPU setups typical of biomedical research groups. Each of the 62 models 134 

was tasked with generating summaries for the 1,000 abstracts in the dataset, resulting in a total 135 

of 62,000 generated summaries for evaluation. 136

Prompt Design 137 

We used an identical prompt across all prompt-based models to ensure compatibility, asking for 138 

concise summaries focused on the main findings of each publication while excluding unneces-139 

sary background or methodological details. The prompt was developed iteratively on a small 140 

number of sample abstracts and then frozen. Each model received the publication title and ab-141 

stract as an input and was asked to produce an output of 15–100 words. A post-hoc check 142 

confirmed that models adhered well to this length constraint, with the large majority of sum-143 

maries falling within the target range across methods. A full per-method breakdown is provided 144 

in Supplementary Figure 7. If the abstract did not contain any substantive results or conclu-145 

sions, the model was instructed to return the predefined token INSUFFICIENT FINDINGS. This 146 

gave models an explicit exit path for cases where an abstract contained no actual findings to 147 

summarize, preventing them from fabricating content simply to fill the response. 148 

The exact prompt used for all models was as follows: 149 

Summarize the provided publication (title and abstract) in 15-100 words.

Evaluation Metrics 150 

As there is no single metric that can fully reflect summary quality, especially in the biomedical 151 

field where both coverage of key information and factual correctness are critical, we employed 152 

both surface-level metrics based on lexical overlap, referred to as lexical-based metrics, and 153 

embedding-level metrics. The latter includes metrics based on semantic similarity, denoted as 154 

semantic-based metrics, as well as metrics that evaluate factual consistency. 155 

Surface-level Metrics 156 

Surface-level metrics compare the generated summaries with the reference summaries mainly 157 

at the word or phrase level. While they do not capture meaning beyond surface overlap, they 158 

remain common metrics in summarization research and provide a straightforward foundation for 159 

evaluation. We used three recall-oriented understudy for gisting evaluation (ROUGE) variants 160 

(ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L) 40 , bilingual evaluation understudy (BLEU) 41 , and metric for 161 

evaluation of translation with explicit ordering (METEOR) 42 . ROUGE-1 and ROUGE-2 measure 162 

how many unigrams (single words) or bigrams (word pairs) from the reference appear in the 163 

generated output, while ROUGE-L identifies the longest sequence of words shared between 164 

the two. BLEU calculates how many n-grams in the output also occur in the reference, em-165 

phasizing precision over recall and applying a brevity penalty to counteract the tendency toward 166 

overly short summaries. METEOR extends n-gram matching by considering word stems and 167 

synonyms, making it more robust to wording variations. Together, these metrics offer a simple 168 

but transparent point of reference. 169 

Embedding-based Metrics 170 

To capture similarity beyond surface-level word overlap, we included a set of embedding-based 171 

metrics built on pre-trained transformer models. These methods generate vector representations 172 

of text, allowing them to capture semantic similarity rather than just word overlap. We measured 173 

semantic similarity using BERT-score 43 , which compares two texts by matching their contextual 174 

token embeddings and averaging the resulting cosine similarities. Because BERT-score needs 175 

a pre-trained encoder to produce these embeddings, we ran it with two well-established choices: 176 

Robustly optimized BERT approach (RoBERTa) 44 and decoding-enhanced BERT with disentan-177 

gled attention (DeBERTa) 45 . This lets the metrics recognize when two summaries express the 178 

same content even if the wording differs. 179 

We further included all-mpnet-base-v2 46 , a transformer model fine-tuned for sentence sim-180 

ilarity. Unlike BERT-score, which matches summaries token by token, masked and permuted 181 

pre-training (MPNet) was trained with a focus on alignment at the sentence-level. This charac-182 

teristic makes it a useful complement to the other metrics, as it is particularly sensitive to whether 183 

the overall meaning of a reference summary is preserved in the system output. 184

Overall Performance Metric 193 

To comprehensively assess the performance of each model on the summarization task, we em-194 

ployed a multi-metric framework covering three dimensions: lexical (n=5), semantic (n=3), and 195 

factual (n=4). To prevent the impact of dimension imbalance, we computed an average score for 196 

each dimension as follows: 197       

> avg lex = ROUGE -1 + ROUGE -2 + ROUGE -L + METEOR + BLEU 5(1)
> avg sem = RoBERTa + DeBERTa + all -mpnet -base -v2 3(2)
> avg fact = AlignScore + SummaC + MiniCheck -FT5 + MiniCheck -7B 4(3)

To examine how the different evaluation metrics relate to each other, we computed pairwise 198 

Spearman correlation coefficients across all models (Figure 2). Averaging metrics within each 199 

dimension ensured that highly correlated measures such as ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-200 

L do not dominate the overall evaluation. 201 

As the different metric dimensions operate on different value ranges, we applied z-score 202 

normalization to the dimension-wise averages across all models, ensuring that lexical, semantic, 203 

and factual metrics contribute comparably to the final evaluation. 204             

> Zlex = avg lex −mean(avg lex )std(avg lex )(4)
> Zsem = avg sem −mean(avg sem )std(avg sem )(5)
> Zfact = avg fact −mean(avg fact )std(avg fact )(6)

The Overall Performance Score (OPS) was computed as the mean of the normalized dimen-205 

sion scores: 206       

> OPS = Zlex +Zsem +Zfact

We finally ranked models by overall performance, resulting in the performance rank, with 207 

lower ranks indicating better performance. To examine which model performed best in each 208

Differences in overall performance between model categories and families were assessed 211 

using Welch’s analysis of variance (ANOVA), followed by pairwise post-hoc comparisons with 212 

Games-Howell tests. This procedure is well suited for groups with unequal sample sizes and 213

models within each category and family. Adjusted p-values were used to determine the statistical 215

Data Leakage Analysis 217 

To assess whether benchmark articles were part of the models’ pretraining data, we conducted 218 

a data leakage analysis by comparing their publication dates against reported model training 219 

cutoff dates. If an article was published before a model’s training cutoff date, it may have been 220 

included in the model’s internal knowledge, potentially giving the model access to information 221 

beyond the input provided (title and abstract). 222 

Publication dates for all benchmark articles were retrieved using the Crossref API. For each 223 

model, the reported training cutoff date was collected from official model documentation (i.e., 224 

provider-released model descriptions) or, when unavailable, from community-curated sources 50 .225 

In total, 21 cutoff dates were confirmed from primary sources, 9 were obtained from community 226 

sources, 17 were estimated based on publicly available information (e.g., Hugging Face docu-227 

mentation or associated publications), and 13 (primarily from newer and less-well documented 228 

models) remained unknown. We then compared article publication dates with model cutoff dates 229 

to determine the proportion of benchmark articles published on or before each model’s training 230 

cutoff. This proportion represents the theoretical maximum share of benchmark articles that 231 

could have been present in the model’s training data. 232 

To further investigate models with high potential overlap, we performed an abstract com-233 

pletion probe. Notably, all models included in this abstract completion probe had cutoff dates 234 

confirmed from either official or community sources. In this test, models were prompted in com-235 

pletion mode with the first sentence of an article abstract and asked to generate the continuation. 236 

For each model, this procedure was done separately for abstracts published before and after 237 

the model’s training cutoff date. The similarity between generated summaries and the original 238 

abstracts were measured using ROUGE-L scores, as ROUGE-L is based on the longest com-239 

mon subsequence (LCS), which makes it sensitive to long in-order token overlaps. If models 240 

had memorized benchmark articles during training, completions for pre-cutoff abstracts would 241 

be expected to show significantly higher similarity scores compared to post-cutoff abstracts. 242 

Differences in ROUGE-L similarity between pre- and post-cutoff articles were assessed using 243 

one-sided Mann-Whitney U tests. 244 

This two-stage analysis provides a practical estimation of whether benchmark articles have 245 

influenced model outputs through previous exposure during pretraining. 246 

Expert Assessment 247 

To assess whether automatic evaluation metrics align with human judgments, we conducted an 248 

expert evaluation of the generated summaries. Eight evaluators with professional backgrounds 249 

in biomedical research and drug discovery participated. A web-based questionnaire was devel-250 

oped and evaluators were asked to rate generated summaries using the four quality dimensions 251 

defined in the SummEval framework 51 , a widely adopted standard for summarization evaluation 252 

that has also been used in recent LLM-based evaluation frameworks such as G-Eval 52 . The 253 

evaluated dimensions were: coherence (logical structure and organization of the summary), flu-254 

ency (grammatical correctness and readability), relevance (coverage of important information), 255 

and consistency (factual alignment with the reference). Each dimension was rated on a five-point 256 

Likert scale ranging from 1 to 5. 257 

The assessment was conducted on summaries generated by the two highest-ranked and the 258 

two lowest-ranked models identified in the automated benchmark: mistral-small3.2:24b, mistral-259 

small-2506, bigbird-pegasus-large-pubmed, and mT5 multilingual XLSum. For evaluation, one 260 

article was randomly selected from each journal category, resulting in 20 biomedical articles and 261 

80 assessments per expert. 262

lexical and semantic evaluation metrics. 270 

In the second step, evaluators were shown the title and abstract of the original article along-271

This mirrors the input used for the automatic factuality metrics, enabling direct comparison. 273 

Inter-rater agreement was quantified using Gwet’s AC2 coefficient 53 with quadratic weights, 274 

chosen for its robustness to skewed score distributions and capturing absolute agreement, to-275 

gether with the mean pairwise Spearman’s ρ, capturing agreement in relative ranking. Pairwise 276 

differences in ratings between models were tested using Mann-Whitney U tests with Bonferroni 277 

correction for multiple comparisons. 278 

LLM-as-a-judge evaluation 279 

We complement the evaluation metrics with an LLM-as-a-judge approach 54 using three models 280 

from distinct providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Mistral) in a panel-of-evaluators design 55 to reduce 281 

intra-model bias relative to any single judge. We deliberately used mid-tier models while limit-282 

ing reasoning efforts, rather than using each provider’s flagship model, to prevent overthinking. 283 

Each judge emitted a brief rationale before scoring (explain-then-rate, which aids alignment with 284 

human ratings 56 ), rating each of the four rubric dimensions on a 1 to 5 scale via forced structured 285 

outputs and receiving the same evidence as the human experts (title, abstract, reference high-286 

lights, and the summary). The panel score for each summary-dimension is the median across 287 

the three judges. This test was introduced as a bias-control and robustness measure rather 288 

than as a source of additional independent signal (see Limitations). We validated the judge 289 

panel against the 80 expert assessments using summary-level Spearman correlation and then 290 

applied it to all 62 models on the same 20 publications (one per journal), rather than the full 291 

1,000-document benchmark. All correlations use scipy.stats.spearmanr .292 

Benchmarking Framework 293 

The benchmark framework, including all downstream statistical tests and supplementary anal-294 

yses, was implemented in Python 3.12. Gold standard data were retrieved from open-access 295 

articles published by ScienceDirect and Cell Press through manual extraction of titles, abstracts, 296 

and highlights sections, along with metadata including publication URLs, identifiers, section 297 

types, and article types where available. Extraction followed an identical procedure for both 298 

publishers, and each record was checked for completeness of the required fields. All data were 299 

stored in machine-readable JSON format. 300 

The framework was implemented using the Python standard library supplemented by sev-301 

eral specialized packages: pandas 57 for data import and export, scikit-learn 58 for computing 302 

cosine similarities of embeddings and TF-IDF vectors, networkx 59 for graph construction and 303 

the PageRank algorithm 60 . Additional evaluation metrics were computed using NLTK 61 for ME-304 

TEOR and BLEU scores, ROUGE-score, BERT-score 43 , AlignScore 47 , the summac package 305

# Results 322 

Our benchmark results offer a comparative view of summarization performance across all eval-323 

uated models. We first examined the correlations among the chosen evaluation metrics and, 324 

based on the findings, identified the best-performing model across lexical, semantic, and factual 325 

dimensions. Next, we compared model performance across categories and families to identify 326 

significant differences. We additionally performed a data leakage analysis to ensure that inclu-327 

sion of benchmarking data in training data had no impact on the results. We then analyzed how 328 

well automatic metrics aligned with human judgment based on expert level result assessment, 329 

and finally assessed whether an LLM-as-a-judge panel could reproduce these rankings. 330 

Metric Correlations 331 

Pairwise Spearman correlations were computed to analyze the relationships between the dif-332 

ferent evaluation metrics (Figure 2). Strong positive correlations were observed among most 333 

lexical-based metrics (ROUGE-1/2/L, METEOR, and BLEU), with the correlation between METEOR 334 

and BLEU marking an exception ( ρ = 0 .23 ). ROUGE variants showed almost identical behavior 335 

(ρ ≥ 0.88 ), while BLEU and METEOR demonstrated slightly weaker but still substantial alignment 336 

with ROUGE measures ( ρ = 0 .51 − 0.82 ). 337 

Most semantic-based metrics (RoBERTa, DeBERTa, and all-mpnet-base-v2) showed high in-338 

ternal consistency ( ρ > 0.5), reflecting their shared focus on semantic similarity. When compared 339 

with lexical-based metrics, correlations were moderate to strong in most cases, indicating that 340 

both categories capture related but not identical dimensions of summary quality. 341 

Concerning factual consistency metrics, AlignScore and the two MiniCheck variants exhibited 342 

very strong correlations with each other ( ρ = 0 .82 − 0.94 ), while SummaC showed moderately 343 

positive correlations with the other factual metrics ( ρ = 0 .41 − 0.69 ). This likely reflects method-344 

ological differences, as SummaC relies on zero-shot natural language inference over sentence 345 

pairs, while MiniCheck and AlignScore use models specifically optimized for factual consistency 346 

assessment. When compared with lexical- and semantic-based metrics, correlations were gen-347 

erally weaker and, in some cases, even slightly negative. This pattern can largely be attributed 348 

to the different point of reference used by factual metrics. 349

Cutoff Overlap Results 415 

Across the evaluated models, potential overlap ranged from minimal levels of 5% for earlier 416 

models with training cutoffs prior to 2024, up to 40.2% for more recent LLMs with reported cutoff 417 

dates in early 2025. Most models showed low overlap ( ≤ 20% ), indicating that the majority 418 

of benchmark articles were published after their respective training cutoff dates. A subset of 419 

eight models, including recent versions of Claude, Deepseek, and one GPT model, exhibited 420 

moderate levels of potential overlap ( 24 − 40 .2% ) and were further analyzed via an abstract 421 

completion probe in the next step. 422 

A complete overview of training cutoff dates and corresponding potential overlap values for 423 

all models is provided in Supplementary Table 8. 424 

Abstract Completion Probe 425 

To assess whether high potential overlap translated into memorization effects, we conducted an 426 

abstract completion probe for the eight models showing moderate levels of potential overlap. For 427 

each model ROUGE-L scores between generated completions and the original abstracts were 428 

compared for articles published before and after the respective training cutoff dates. 429 

Average ROUGE-L scores were nearly identical for pre-cutoff and post-cutoff articles (both at 430 

approximately 13.5%) and there were only negligible differences at the individual model level (Ta-431 

ble 3). Statistical testing using a one-sided Mann-Whitney U test revealed no significant increase 432 

in similarity for pre-cutoff articles in any of the tested models. Effect sizes were consistently close 433 

to zero, which further indicates that there is no systematic difference between the two groups. 434 

If memorization effects had occurred, substantially higher similarity scores would be expected 435 

for abstracts from pre-cutoff articles. As this was not the case, we concluded that the models 436 

were not reproducing memorized content from their training data. 437

# Discussion 489 

Our benchmarking analysis of 62 text summarization models revealed clear performance dif-490 

ferences. General-purpose LLMs achieved the highest summarization quality across all metric 491 

dimensions, closely followed by general-purpose SLMs, domain-specific LLMs, and reasoning-492 

oriented LLMs. In contrast, domain-specific SLMs, encoder–decoder architectures, and tradi-493 

tional extractive methods performed significantly worse regarding overall performance. These 494 

results highlight the clear progression from extractive and encoder–decoder approaches toward 495 

transformer-based models, while also showing that domain-specific fine-tuning alone does not 496 

necessarily lead to improved summarization quality. 497 

Comparing models by architecture, size, and domain focus shows that, overall, LLMs perform 498 

best, likely due to their large number of parameters, which enable them to better understand the 499 

complex context typical for biomedical literature. While lightweight models like Gemma3:270M 500 

may lack the capacity to handle this complexity, SLMs remain competitive, with some models, 501 

such as mistral:7b and granite4:tiny-h, even outperforming several LLMs across the three metric 502 

dimensions. This may be attributed to the fact that smaller datasets are often more curated and 503 

of higher quality compared to the large amount of data required to train a large model 66 .504 

Interestingly, medium-sized models (e.g., many models in the Mistral family) outperform larger 505 

proprietary ones. These models seem to reach an optimal balance between parameter count 506 

and performance, where additional parameters could disrupt this equilibrium, potentially leading 507 

to over-fitting or plateaus 67 .508 

We also found that overall general-purpose models outperform both domain-specific models 509 

specialized in the biomedical domain and those specialized for text summarization, regardless 510 

of size. A possible explanation might be that domain-specific models fine-tuned on biomedical 511

for text summarization might be good at summarizing in general but fail at capturing the com-514

Most reasoning-oriented models ranked in the middle, indicating moderate performance. This 520 

likely reflects their multi-step reasoning nature, which can be advantageous for tasks that require 521 

breaking down problems into sequential steps, such as mathematical operations or computer 522 

programming, but seems less suited for text summarization, which requires semantic compres-523 

sion and factual grounding 70 .524 

Traditional models ranked highest on the factual dimension, likely because their extractive ap-525 

proach generates summaries closely aligned with the source abstracts, which factuality metrics 526

The expert assessment supported the findings of the automated benchmark. The two highest-528 

ranked models consistently outperformed the two lowest-ranked models across all four evalua-529 

tion dimensions. This alignment suggests that the automatic metrics capture relevant aspects of 530 

summary quality and are broadly consistent with human judgments. 531 

The results of this benchmark provide useful guidance for selecting summarization models 532 

in biomedical and scientific settings. The strong performance of general-purpose LMs indicates 533 

that broad, diverse pretraining is often more advantageous than narrow domain adaptation when 534 

dealing with unseen scientific content. 535 

Another key consideration is the trade-off between output quality and processing efficiency. 536 

While LLMs achieved the highest overall scores, SLMs deliver competitive results at substantially 537 

lower computational cost 72 , which makes them especially attractive for large-scale or resource-538 

constrained applications. Choosing between large and small models therefore depends not only 539 

on desired output quality but also on the intended scale of summarization. 540 

Overall, the findings suggest that general-purpose LMs currently offer the most reliable and 541 

practical choice for biomedical text summarization. Their consistent performance across eval-542 

uation criteria demonstrates that broad generalization outweighs the marginal gains from more 543 

narrowly specialized or fine-tuned approaches, many of which are not primarily optimized for text 544 

summarization. 545 

Limitations of the study 546 

Even though our results are based on a robust evaluation framework, several limitations of our 547 

study warrant discussion. Model access methods varied across the evaluation due to differing 548 

application programming interface (API) capabilities and requirements. Hugging Face models 549 

were accessed through their supported interfaces: the pipeline API with task=”summarization” 550 

where available, or chat/completion formats for models that did not support the pipeline ap-551 

proach. Ollama models required use of the generate endpoint with merged prompts, while 552 

OpenAI, Anthropic, and Mistral models each mandated their respective provider-specific APIs 553 

(responses.create, messages.create, and chat.complete) with distinct message structures. We 554 

applied hyperparameter normalization where possible, though API-level constraints prevented 555 

full standardization. For example, GPT-5 does not support temperature control, instead offering 556 

only reasoning-specific parameters. Additionally, proprietary middleware layers may transform 557 

requests and responses in undocumented ways, potentially affecting outputs independently of 558

Reference-based evaluation cannot fully capture every valid way a good summary might be 566 

written, as summarization is largely a subjective exercise. To account for this, we tried to capture 567 

summary quality along diverse, complementary dimensions by combining lexical, semantic, and 568 

factual metrics, and as a further assessment included a blinded expert evaluation as an indepen-569 

dent check on the automatic scores. Nonetheless, since automatic metrics and human ratings 570 

have not fully aligned in other settings 5,6 , we cannot formally guarantee that our metrics capture 571 

the full space of valid summaries, which remains an important direction for future work. 572 

Regarding our LLM-as-a-judge results, it is notable that the three judges are not indepen-573 

dent: correlated errors mean the panel yields far fewer effective votes than its size suggests, 574 

and a strong single judge can match it 75 . We therefore treat the panel median as bias-control, 575 

not a route to higher accuracy, and its edge over the human leave-one-out ceiling reflects me-576 

dian smoothing rather than above-human reliability. Furthermore, the summary-level agreement 577 

pools models of very different quality. Within-model agreement is much lower, so the judge ranks 578 

models reliably but separates similar-quality summaries weakly. Finally, the system-level agree-579 

ment compares two automatic methods, not validation against humans (infeasible with only four 580 

expert-rated models). 581 

Another limitation of this benchmarking study is that we focused on a single summarization 582 

task: generating concise summaries from biomedical abstracts. This setup provides a clear and 583 

well-defined evaluation framework, but the findings may not fully extend to other forms of sci-584 

entific or biomedical summarization, including full-text articles, clinical trial data, or lay-oriented 585 

summaries. Given the rapid evolution of LLMs, these results just capture a specific snapshot in 586 

time and may change as newer architectures and models become available. 587 

A related consideration is our choice of reference summary. We used author-written high-588 

lights as the reference against which summaries were evaluated, but condensing an abstract (let 589 

alone a full publication) into a few bullet points is inherently subjective, and highlights inevitably 590 

emphasize some findings over others. We nonetheless regard them as the most suitable refer-591 

ence available, since they are author-generated, standardized across both publishers, and well 592 

matched to our target of concise summaries within limited length. 593 

Finally, although we deliberately drew articles from 20 journals spanning a broad range of 594 

biomedical and translational fields, our corpus inevitably covers only part of this very diverse 595 

domain. We cannot rule out that summarization performance, and the resulting model rankings, 596 

would differ for subfields not well presented in our selection, leaving open how well these results 597 

generalize across the biomedical domain. 598 

# Conclusion 599 

This benchmark provides a comprehensive evaluation of 62 text summarization methods on a 600 

dataset of 1,000 biomedical abstracts paired with standardized highlights sections. Across all 601 

lexical overlap, semantic similarity, and factual consistency metrics, general-purpose LLMs were 602 

the best-performing models. Their ability to incorporate broad pretraining with strong semantic 603 

understanding enabled them to outperform reasoning-oriented models, domain-specific mod-604 

els, and encoder-decoder architectures. Especially medium-sized models achieved the highest 605
