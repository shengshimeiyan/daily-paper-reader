# To Answer or to Abstain: Mitigating Search-Agent Hallucinations via Abstention-Aware Reinforcement Learning 

Fengji Zhang 1, Tianyu Fan 2, Yuxiang Zheng 2, Xinyao Niu 2,Chengen Huang 2, Jacky Keung 1, Bei Chen 2

> 1

Abstract 

Recent advances in equipping Large Lan-guage Models (LLMs) with search tools and outcome-reward reinforcement learning (RL) have achieved new state-of-the-art results on open-domain QA tasks. However, we argue that current training paradigms harbor a critical vulnerability: they predominantly reward cor-rect answers but fail to penalize fabricated ones when retrieval fails, thereby implicitly exacer-bating hallucinations. To address this, we pro-pose Abstention-Aware Reinforcement Learn-ing ( AWA-RL ), which dynamically shapes the abstention reward utilizing the modelвҖҷs query-specific prior capabilities and continuous on-policy training observations. We also intro-duce a novel metric, RA-F1 , to measure the capability-reliability trade-off. Compared to non-abstaining baselines, AWA-RL boosts ab-solute precision by up to 10.3% and over-all RA-F1 by 2.9%, with only marginal sacri-fice in raw accuracy. These results confirm that AWA-RL successfully yields highly capa-ble and reliable search agents. The code, data, and model weights are publicly available at 

https://github.com/zfj1998/AWA-RL .

1 Introduction 

Hallucination remains a long-standing and notori-ously difficult challenge in LLMs. It is challeng-ing to ensure the factual grounding of generated output. (Bang et al., 2025; Kalai et al., 2025) A prevailing approach to mitigating hallucinations is providing sufficient context to ground the answers. Progressing from early Retrieval-Augmented Gen-eration methods, (Agrawal et al., 2024; BГ©chard and Ayala, 2024) recent advancements have in-troduced more advanced search agents. (Li et al., 2025b,a) By leveraging outcome-reward RL, these agents learn to interact with search environments iteratively to arrive at ground-truth answers. However, this training paradigm harbors a crit-ical vulnerability: current RL objectives predom-inantly incentivize models to output the correct answer, but lack a mechanism to reward absten-tion. (Jin et al., 2025) Consequently, when an agent encounters queries beyond its capability bound-aries or fails to retrieve sufficient evidence, it tends to fabricate an answer rather than safely abstain. This вҖңover-answeringвҖқ phenomenon could exacer-bate hallucinations. (Song et al., 2025) The critical research question thus arises: How can we equip search agents with the self-awareness to recognize their capability boundaries and confidently abstain from ungrounded answers? 

Learning to abstain is a non-trivial objective. Ex-isting abstention-related studies (Xu et al., 2024b; Song et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2026; Zheng et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024a) primarily suffer from three major limitations 1: (1) Mismatched Scenarios :They predominantly focus on single-turn QA rather than multi-turn agentic settings, where capability boundaries dynamically shift based on tool-use and search results. (2) Degradation of Capabilities :Most approaches employ post-hoc pipeline train-ing, which first trains for capability, then tunes for abstention. This disconnected process often severely compromises the modelвҖҷs original search and reasoning abilities. (3) Reliance on Heuristics :They heavily rely on synthesizing new unanswer-able datasets, which inevitably introduces human heuristics that fail to generalize across models with varying intrinsic capabilities. To address these challenges, we propose AWA-RL 

(Abstention-a WA re Reinforcement Learning). The core intuition behind AWA-RL is that the incentive to abstain should not be a universal constant but rather dynamically calibrated to the modelвҖҷs intrin-sic problem-solving capability for each specific query. At a high level, we first estimate the modelвҖҷs prior success rate for a given question to establish a query-aware refusal baseline. Second, to counter-

act the agentвҖҷs natural bias toward premature вҖңlazy refusalвҖқ, we introduce a non-linear optimistic map-ping controlled by a single, explainable вҖңcourageвҖқ factor. Finally, during the RL training phase, we continuously monitor the deviation between the agentвҖҷs actual abstention behavior and the initial capability estimate, dynamically updating the ab-stention reward in real time. Through extensive experiments, we reveal ex-actly why conventional, intuitive approaches fail to establish a reliable capability-reliability bound-ary. First, simply manipulating data proportions, such as injecting unanswerable queries or refusal trajectories into the training data, results in highly unpredictable behavior. Second, applying a static refusal reward acts as a blunt instrument that ig-nores query difficulty variance. Especially in RL from scratch settings, even a minimal static reward triggers catastrophic reward hacking. In contrast, AWA-RL cleanly overcomes these lim-itations. Compared to standard non-abstaining RL baselines, our method dramatically boosts abso-lute precision by up to 10.3% and improves overall 

RA-F1 by 5.2%, at the cost of only a marginal drop in raw accuracy. Crucially, empirical results con-firm that the вҖңcourageвҖқ factor is remarkably easy to tune, and the policyвҖҷs abstention behavior is highly responsive to its adjustments. In conclusion, the dynamic reward mechanism of AWA-RL provides a vastly more robust and scalable pathway toward genuinely reliable search agents.

2.2 Outcome-Reward RL Preliminaries 

SOTA search agents are predominantly trained us-ing outcome-reward RL, optionally preceded by cold-start SFT. During RL, the environment pro-vides a sparse binary reward R(y, y вҲ—) вҲҲ { 0, 1},indicating whether the final answer y matches the ground truth yвҲ—. G trajectories and optimizes the policy using the relative advantage Ai, computed by normalizing the outcome rewards within the group.

3 Experimental Setup 

Datasets & Implementaion Details. We eval-uate on three challenging multi-hop QA bench-marks: HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMulti-HopQA (Ho et al., 2020), and MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022). Our primary experiments utilize 

Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2024) as the base model. To establish the cold-start setting, the model is first fine-tuned on 5,000 search-augmented reasoning trajectories generated by a stronger teacher model ( GPT-oss-120b 2). This

bootstraps the agentвҖҷs initial multi-step search ca-pabilities while preserving sufficient action-space entropy. Subsequently, we conduct reinforcement learning via GRPO on 5,000 filtered queries sam-pled from the training splits. The agent inter-acts with a standard Wikipedia search environment. During the RL phase, we employ GPT-oss-120b 

as the external LLM judge to automatically evalu-ate both final answer correctness and intermediate citation quality. More implementation details can be found in Appendix C. 

Evaluation Metrics. To comprehensively assess the capability-reliability trade-off, let Nc, Nr, and 

Nw denote the number of correct, refused (ab-stained), and wrong responses, respectively, where 

Ntotal = Nc + Nr + Nw. Following the evaluation principles of Xu et al. (2024b), we measure policy performance using three metrics. Accuracy (Acc) ,defined as Nc/N total , evaluates the modelвҖҷs overall helpfulness and serves as our Recall. Precision (Prec) , computed as Nc/(Nc + Nw), reflects the modelвҖҷs self-knowledge by measuring correctness strictly when it chooses to answer. Finally, we in-troduce Reliability-Aware F1 ( RA-F1 ). Calculated as the harmonic mean of Precision and Accuracy (RA-F1 = 2 В· Prec В· Acc /(Prec + Acc )), this single metric holistically captures the optimal balance be-tween answering confidently and abstaining safely. 

Baselines. We compare AWA-RL against three rep-resentative abstention paradigms. SFT-Mixed in-jects 10%вҖ“30% teacher-generated refusal trajec-tories into the SFT data to test supervised elicita-tion. RL-Unanswerable mixes 10%вҖ“30% inher-ently unanswerable queries into the RL training set, optimizing via standard binary-reward GRPO. Finally, Static Reward applies RL with a fixed refusal reward rref (e.g., 0.5 or 0.9), explicitly dis-couraging answers unless the modelвҖҷs confidence exceeds this static threshold.

5 Conclusion 

We introduced AWA-RL to resolve the critical capability-reliability trade-off in LLM agents. While conventional methods, such as data manip-ulation or static rewards, inevitably trigger stub-born overconfidence or catastrophic reward hack-ing, AWA-RL dynamically anchors the refusal re-ward to the modelвҖҷs query-specific prior capabil-ities. Controlled by a single, highly responsive вҖңcourageвҖқ factor ( Оі), our framework successfully decouples capability acquisition from abstention calibration. By maximizing overall system util-ity ( RA-F1 ), AWA-RL provides a robust and scalable pathway toward genuinely reliable search agents. Limitations 

While AWA-RL demonstrates significant improve-ments in optimizing the capability-reliability trade-off, we acknowledge a few limitations that pave the way for future research. 

Scale and Task Modality. Our current empir-ical evaluations are primarily focused on the 

Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct model and multi-hop agen-tic search QA tasks. Although the theoretical for-mulation of our dynamic reward shaping is inher-ently model-agnostic and broadly applicable to general agentic RL domains (e.g., complex tool-use, mathematical reasoning, and coding), scaling the validation to larger foundational models and a wider spectrum of task modalities remains an im-portant next step to confirm its universal efficacy. 

Dependence on the Courage Factor. AWA-RL 

successfully distills the complex balance between answering and abstaining into a single, intuitive hyperparameter: the courage factor Оі. As demon-strated in our training dynamics, the policy is highly responsive to Оі, making it practically straightforward to tune. Nevertheless, identifying the strictly optimal value still requires empirical adjustments based on specific dataset difficulties and model initializations. Our future research will explore fully end-to-end, adaptive mechanisms that can auto-calibrate this confidence mapping without human intervention. 

Computational Overhead for Prior Estima-tion. Establishing the optimal base refusal reward (Rbase ) requires a preliminary inference pass over the training set to calculate the prior success rate (pi). While this offline sampling introduces an initial computational cost, we argue it is a highly worthwhile trade-off: it provides the most faithful and unbiased estimation of the modelвҖҷs intrinsic capabilities, which is the cornerstone for precise refusal boundary control during RL. Still, develop-ing sample-free or computationally lighter proxy metrics to estimate model capability presents an exciting avenue for subsequent optimization. 

Ethical Considerations 

Our work primarily contributes to the safety and reliability of Large Language Models by explic-itly training them to abstain from answering when sufficient evidence is lacking. By addressing the capability-reliability trade-off in agentic QA sys-tems, our AWA-RL framework directly mitigates the risks of hallucination and overconfident factual er-rors, which is a crucial step toward deploying trust-worthy AI in real-world scenarios. Despite these positive impacts, several ethical implications warrant careful consideration: 

Disparities in Abstention. In our framework, the abstention boundary is intrinsically anchored to the modelвҖҷs prior capability estimation ( pi). It is well-documented that pre-trained language models often exhibit performance disparities, generally perform-ing worse on topics related to underrepresented de-mographics, minority cultures, or low-resource lan-guages. Consequently, our dynamic reward shap-ing might disproportionately encourage the agent to refuse queries from these marginalized domains. While abstaining is safer than hallucinating, this behavior could inadvertently exacerbate inequali-ties in information access, as users querying non-mainstream topics might consistently receive вҖңnot enough informationвҖқ responses. 

Bias in External Knowledge and Evaluators. 

Our agentвҖҷs search and reasoning capabilities are optimized interacting with an external corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) and supervised by an LLM-based judge for citation rewards. Any inherent biases, historical prejudices, or misinformation embedded within the retrieval environment can shape the agentвҖҷs defi-nition of вҖңtruth.вҖқ Furthermore, LLM judges may harbor implicit preferences for certain linguistic styles or mainstream viewpoints. If the judge in-correctly penalizes valid but minority perspectives, the policy might learn to improperly reject valid queries or confidently output biased information. Future deployments of abstention-aware agents should pair our dynamic reward mechanisms with rigorous bias audits, ensuring equitable capability expansion across diverse user groups and topics.

A Related Work 

Agentic Search for Open-domain QA. Recent progress in open-domain QA has increasingly shifted from one-shot retrieval-augmented gener-ation to agentic search , where LLMs interleave reasoning with explicit actions such as query refor-mulation, retrieval, and evidence integration. Early paradigms such as ReAct (Yao et al., 2022) and Toolformer (Schick et al., 2023) established the value of coupling reasoning with external actions and tool use, showing that iterative interaction with external knowledge sources can substantially im-prove factuality and task performance. Building on this line, more recent search and deep research agents train models to perform multi-turn retrieval and synthesis in a more end-to-end manner. For example, Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) optimizes in-terleaved search-and-reason trajectories with rein-forcement learning and a simple outcome-based re-ward, while recent deep research systems combine iterative web search with preference tuning, super-vised fine-tuning, or RL to improve long-horizon reasoning and report generation (Yamada et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2025; Fan et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). These advances have significantly strength-ened the capability frontier of search-based QA agents. However, existing agentic search training paradigms are still largely correctness-centric . In representative RL formulations such as Search-R1, the reward is defined only over the final outcome correctness, with no explicit incentive for refus-ing unsupported answers (Jin et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a; Team et al., 2025; Chu et al., 2026; Tao et al., 2025; Feng et al., 2025). Likewise, recent deep research systems primarily optimize answer quality, factuality, or task success, rather than cali-brating when the agent should abstain. As a result, once retrieval fails, evidence is insufficient, or the problem lies beyond the agentвҖҷs effective capability boundary, the policy is still biased toward produc-ing an answer. Our work addresses precisely this missing dimension: instead of treating abstention as a post-hoc patch, we incorporate it directly into the same RL process that trains search behavior, so that capability improvement and reliability calibra-tion are optimized jointly rather than separately. 

Abstention Training. A growing body of work studies how to make LLMs refuse questions that fall outside their knowledge boundary. One line of research formulates refusal as a reliability-alignment problem. RLKF (Xu et al., 2024b) trains a reward model from knowledge feedback so that refusal is preferred on out-of-knowledge questions, while EKBM (Zheng et al., 2025) ex-plicitly models knowledge boundaries through a fast-slow framework that refines uncertain cases with an additional reasoning stage. Another line focuses on the adverse effect of capability-oriented post-training: Song et al. (2025) show that standard reinforcement finetuning can sharply reduce refusal behavior and increase hallucination on synthetic unanswerable math problems, and Kirichenko et al. (2025) further show that modern reasoning models often exhibit degraded abstention despite higher reasoning accuracy. More task-specific approaches include training abstention on temporal QA with CoT supervision and abstention-aware RL (Zhou et al., 2026), as well as reliability alignment for tool use by expanding the action space with indecisive actions and optimizing on synthesized data (Xu et al., 2024a). Despite their promise, these methods leave sev-eral open issues for search agents. First, most prior abstention work is developed in single-turn or otherwise non-agentic settings, such as math QA, temporal QA, dialogue state tracking, or tool se-lection, where the modelвҖҷs capability boundary is comparatively static (Xu et al., 2024b; Song et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2026; Zheng et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024a). In contrast, for search agents, the answerability of a question changes dynamically as the agent interacts with the environment and accumulates evidence. Second, many approaches rely on synthesized unanswerable data, abstained variants of existing benchmarks, or auxiliary re-ward models/modules, which may introduce task-specific heuristics and reduce portability across models with different intrinsic capabilities (Song et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2026; Xu et al., 2024b,a). Third, existing reward designs often treat absten-tion with a fixed bonus or through a separate post-hoc stage, which makes it difficult to preserve the modelвҖҷs willingness to answer when sufficient evidence is actually available (Zhou et al., 2026; Zheng et al., 2025). Our proposed AWA-RL is de-signed to fill this gap: it learns abstention within 

search-agent RL, uses a dynamically shaped refusal reward tied to an estimated prior capability rather than a static refusal bonus, and adds an explicit generation-encouragement term to avoid collapse into lazy refusal. In this way, our method targets the core unresolved problem left by prior work: how to jointly optimize search capability and ab-stention reliability under a unified, lightweight RL objective. 

B Additional Recipes for Stable RL 

In this section, we provide detailed descriptions of the auxiliary training mechanisms utilized to sta-bilize the reinforcement learning process and to continuously enhance the underlying search capa-bilities of the agent. 

B.1 Reward Stabilization via EMA Smoothing and Step-wise Updates 

During the on-policy rollout phase, the empirical refusal rate ( ЛҶОјref ) can fluctuate significantly from step to step simply due to the random sampling of prompts and the inherent stochasticity of the modelвҖҷs generation. If the dynamic penalty P (Et)

is updated immediately based on these noisy step-wise fluctuations, the reward landscape will be-come overly jittery, leading to unstable policy up-dates and preventing the model from converging to an optimal abstention boundary. To mitigate this variance, we introduce two ro-bust stabilization mechanisms: 1. Exponential Moving Average (EMA) Smoothing: Instead of using the instanta-neous excess refusal rate Et, we maintain a smoothed running average ВҜEt across training steps: 

ВҜEt = Оұ ВҜEtвҲ’1 + (1 вҲ’ Оұ)Et

where Оұ вҲҲ [0 , 1) is the decay factor (set to 

Оұ = 0 .5 in our experiments). This EMA acts as a low-pass filter, ensuring that the penalty only responds to sustained trends in over-refusal or under-refusal rather than tran-sient batch noise. 2. Step-wise Quantization: To further prevent microscopic, continuous shifts in the refusal reward, we enforce discrete, step-wise up-dates by quantizing the smoothed excess rate 

ВҜEt. Given a predefined step-tier threshold П„

(e.g., П„ = 0 .1), the effective excess rate is discretized as: 

ЛңEt = П„ вҢҠ ВҜEt/П„ вҢү

where вҢҠВ·вҢү denotes rounding to the nearest in-teger. Ultimately, the dynamic penalty is calculated as 

P ( ЛңEt) rather than P (Et). This quantization cre-ates a вҖңstaircaseвҖқ effect: the final refusal reward

B.2 Dense Citation Reward and Composite Objective 

Relying exclusively on the sparse outcome reward, which assigns 1 for correct, 0 for incorrect, and rref 

for abstention, can be highly inefficient for training complex agentic tasks. When the model receives a 

0, it lacks granular feedback on why it failed (e.g., did it fail to retrieve the right documents, or did it fail to reason over them?). To provide a dense learning signal that continu-ously bolsters the modelвҖҷs multi-hop search and rea-soning skills, we introduce an LLM-judged citation reward. During generation, the agent is prompted to cite specific supporting document IDs for its claims. An external, powerful LLM acts as a judge to evaluate these citations across two dimensions: вҖў Citation Coverage ( Rcov ): The judge eval-uates whether the cited documents contain all the necessary logical hops to derive the final answer, outputting a continuous score 

Rcov вҲҲ [0 , 1] .вҖў Citation Redundancy ( Rred ): To prevent вҖңci-tation hackingвҖқ (where the agent trivially cites every retrieved document to maximize cover-age), the judge simultaneously checks for ir-relevant citations, assigning a discrete penalty score Rred вҲҲ { 0, 0.25 , 0.5, 1}.For the final GRPO update, we combine these dense signals with our sparse outcome reward. To prevent rewards of different scales from dominating the gradient, we independently whiten (z-score nor-malize) each component within the sampled gen-eration group. Let Wh (В·) denote this intra-group whitening operation. The final composite advan-tage Ai for a specific trajectory is formulated as a weighted sum: 

Ai = 2 В·Wh (R(y, y вҲ—, q i))+3 В·Wh (Rcov )вҲ’1В·Wh (Rred )

This composite objective represents a highly ef-fective decoupling strategy. By heavily weighting the dense citation metrics (ratio of 3 for coverage), the agent steadily optimizes its fine-grained search and grounding skills. Meanwhile, the dynamically scaled outcome reward (ratio of 2) is freed up to be exclusively dedicated to balancing the overar-ching capability-reliability (answer-versus-abstain) trade-off. 

C Detailed Experimental Setup 

In this section, we provide the comprehensive im-plementation details, environment configurations, and hyperparameters omitted from the main text. 

C.1 Datasets and Search Environment Evaluation and Training Data. For the main evaluation, we randomly sample 1,000 queries from the official validation sets of HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA (2Wiki), and MuSiQue. For the RL training phase, we utilize the training splits of 2Wiki and MuSiQue. To ensure the efficiency of the GRPO updates, we conduct a preliminary test rollout on the training set to filter out queries that are either trivially easy (where the model answers correctly in all sampled trajectories) or impossi-bly hard (where the model fails in all trajectories). Such queries provide zero relative advantage and contribute little to policy learning. From the re-maining filtered pool, we randomly sample 5,000 queries for the final RL training. 

Search Environment. We adopt a search en-vironment configuration identical to Search-R1. Specifically, we use the 2018 Wikipedia dump, par-titioned into 100-word chunks. These chunks are embedded using the E5 model (Wang et al., 2022) and indexed using FAISS (Douze et al., 2024) for efficient dense retrieval. At each query step, the retriever returns the top-5 passages based on em-bedding cosine similarity. 

C.2 Implementation and Hyperparameters Model Initialization. All primary experiments are conducted using the Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct 

model, which inherently possesses strong founda-tional potential for tool use. For our cold-start setting, we generate 5,000 distinct, successful tra-jectories using a highly capable teacher model (GPT-oss-120b ). The base Qwen model under-goes Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on this dataset for 2 epochs, successfully bootstrapping its search and reasoning skills before RL intervention. 

RL Configuration. During the reinforcement learning phase, we train the model for 5 epochs using the GRPO algorithm. The global batch size is set to 256, the PPO mini-batch size is 256, and the group rollout size ( G) is set to 16. We use a learning rate of 1 Г— 10 вҲ’6. The maximum context length is capped at 16,384 tokens, and the agent is allowed a maximum of 20 interaction turns with the search environment per query. 

Entropy Control and Judges. To maintain ex-ploration and prevent premature policy collapse during RL, we completely remove the standard KL divergence penalty in GRPO. Instead, we apply the dynamic entropy control mechanism introduced in Skywork-OR1 (He et al., 2025), strictly keeping the policy entropy above a threshold of 0.4. Fur-thermore, two separate GPT-oss-120b instances are employed as LLM judges: one dedicated to evaluating the rigorous correctness of the final an-swer, and the other to assessing citation quality (coverage and redundancy). All prompt templates used for the agent and the judges are provided in Appendix D. 

Hardware. All training and evaluation pipelines are executed on two compute nodes, each equipped with 8 Г—NVIDIA H100 GPUs.
