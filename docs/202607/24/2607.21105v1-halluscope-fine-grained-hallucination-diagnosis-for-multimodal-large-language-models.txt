## Abstract 

Although Multimodal Large Language Models have achieved strong performance across a wide range of vision-language tasks, they still suffer from hallucinations, where model outputs become incon-sistent with the visual content, textual context, or commonsense knowledge. Existing studies primarily address this problem through coarse-grained detection. However, these approaches often provide insufficient diagnostic information for understanding hallucina-tion types and supporting downstream hallucination mitigation. To bridge this gap, we propose fine-grained hallucination diagnosis for MLLMs, a new unified task that jointly performs hallucination detection, classification, and interpretable explanation generation. We develop an automated data generation pipeline and construct HalluScope-30K, a large-scale diagnostic dataset covering eight sources and five task categories. Based on this dataset, we design a multi-granular joint reward function and train two diagnosis models, HalluScope-4B and HalluScope-8B, which achieve state-of-the-art performance on both the MHALO benchmark and our fine-grained hallucination classification benchmark. Notably, detection and classification are mutually beneficial under joint optimization. Furthermore, diagnosis-driven feedback experiments show that the fine-grained diagnostic explanations produced by our model effectively guide target models to correct their hallucinations, with full diagnosis substantially outperforming all baselines on both Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct and LLaVA-1.5-7B.

## 2 RELATED WORK 2.1 Hallucination in MLLMs 

Hallucination in multimodal large language models (MLLMs), where the generated content is inconsistent with the visual input, textual context, or established world knowledge [ 8 , 26 ], has attracted in-creasing attention. In MLLMs, such inconsistencies are typically at-tributed to strong language priors that encourage fluent but visually ungrounded continuations, and statistical biases from pre-training data that lead models to rely on spurious correlations [23]. Prior studies have explored hallucination detection by identify-ing inconsistencies between model outputs and visual or textual ev-idence, progressing from coarse-grained response-level judgments to finer-grained analysis such as factual consistency assessment, claim verification, and span-level detection [ 7, 35 , 41 , 50 , 52 ]. In par-allel, another line of research focuses on hallucination mitigation through training-time alignment, external verification, inference-time decoding control, and internal model editing [ 23 , 27 , 38 , 42 , 47 –49 , 54 , 56 ]. While effective mitigation often relies on accurate detec-tion signals, most existing methods treat detection and mitigation as separate stages without providing unified diagnostic feedback that integrates error localization, type classification, and causal ex-planation. Our work addresses this gap by proposing fine-grained hallucination diagnosis as a unified task. 

## 2.2 Fine-grained Hallucination Detection and Mitigation for MLLMs 

Recent studies on hallucination in MLLMs have increasingly moved from coarse-grained response-level judgment toward fine-grained analysis, which aims to provide more precise supervision for hal-lucination detection and mitigation. Early efforts mainly decom-pose model outputs into smaller textual or semantic units, such as clauses, sentences, or claims. For example, M-HalDetect [ 16 ] per-forms clause-level hallucination detection, Xiao et al. [ 46 ] operate at the sentence level, and Pelican [ 35 ] verifies hallucinations at the claim level through claim decomposition and grounding. More recent work pushes fine-grained hallucination detection and mitigation toward token-level modeling [ 4, 10 – 12 , 44 , 57 ]. Whitehead et al. [ 44 ] formulate hallucination detection as a se-quence labeling problem to directly localize hallucinated content at fine granularity. MHALO [ 4 ] further trains a dedicated model for token-level hallucination detection with coarse-grained type classification, but does not provide fine-grained classification or diagnostic explanations for downstream mitigation. In addition, HalluScope: Fine-grained Hallucination Diagnosis for Multimodal Large Language Models Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA 

MetaToken [ 11 ], TLDR [ 12 ], and ECD [ 10 ] estimate token-level hal-lucination likelihood during generation through meta-classification, reward modeling, and decoding-time probability estimation, respec-tively. Meanwhile, MTRE [ 57 ] suggests that hallucination signals may emerge progressively across multiple tokens or semantic levels. Overall, fine-grained hallucination signals are increasingly inte-grated with downstream mitigation. However, existing methods primarily address span-level localization, while hallucination clas-sification remains coarse-grained and diagnostic explanations are largely absent, limiting the actionable value of detection outputs. 

## 3 PRELIMINARY 3.1 Hallucination Diagnosis Task Definition 

We formulate fine-grained hallucination diagnosis as a unified task that encompasses hallucination detection, hallucination classifica-tion, and interpretable explanation generation. Given a multimodal input consisting of an image, a textual query, and the model’s re-sponse, the task aims to localize hallucinated spans in the response, classify each span into a predefined hallucination type, and generate interpretable explanations that describe the causes of hallucination and suggest possible correction strategies. Specifically, for hallucination detection, the model identifies and marks hallucinated spans within the response through structured XML annotations. For hallucination classification, we adopt the taxonomy proposed in MHALO [ 4], which organizes hallucinations into two high-level categories: perception and reasoning . Perception-level hallucinations arise from errors in visual understanding or information extraction from images, whereas reasoning-level hal-lucinations originate from incorrect inference or logical reasoning based on perceived information (the full taxonomy with 12 fine-grained types is provided in Appendix). For explanation generation, the model produces natural language descriptions that explain why each hallucination occurs and suggest how it may be corrected.

## 4 METHOD 

Figure 2 presents the HalluScope framework. Starting from eight datasets across five task categories, the framework generates model responses and constructs fine-grained hallucination labels through two complementary strategies: hallucination injection for correctly answered samples and hallucination annotation for incorrectly an-swered ones. After multi-stage quality control, the framework yields HalluScope-30K with nearly 30,000 diagnostic examples. Based on this dataset, we design a multi-granular joint reward function and train two diagnosis models: HalluScope-4B and HalluScope-8B. 

## 4.1 Pipeline of Data Generation 

4.1.1 Data Collection. The HalluScope framework begins with the collection of eight datasets spanning five task categories: (1) general perception : MMBench [ 28 ] and MMStar [ 5]; (2) mathematical reason-ing : Geo170K [ 13 ] and MathV360K [ 36 ]; (3) ocr : OCRBench [ 29 ]; (4) 

spatial reasoning : RealWorldQA [ 45 ]; and (5) hallucination detection :M-HalDetect [ 16 ] and RLHF-V [ 49 ]. Since these datasets differ in format and annotation, we first perform standardized preprocess-ing: unifying data fields, removing duplicates, and filtering invalid samples (e.g., missing answers). Each processed sample follows the notation 𝑥 𝑘 defined in Section 3.2. Given the unified dataset, we use the base model (before post-training) to generate responses for each sample. Since the original ground-truth answers are typically brief and lack sufficient detail for fine-grained annotation, we regenerate longer, more informa-tive responses to provide richer context for hallucination diagnosis. Unlike approaches that rely on strong closed-source models to ex-pand ground-truth answers, constructing hallucination data from the base model’s actual outputs better preserves the realism of syn-thesized samples, as they reflect the model’s genuine hallucination behaviors rather than artificially simulated ones. This also improves data diversity, since the model exhibits varied hallucination patterns across different tasks and samples. 

4.1.2 Hallucination Label Generation. After obtaining model re-sponses, we construct fine-grained hallucination labels by partition-ing samples based on the consistency between the model response and the ground-truth answer. For correctly answered samples ,we employ a closed-source model (Gemini-3-Pro) to perform hallu-cination injection : analyzing the original response and introducing targeted modifications tailored to specific hallucination types while maintaining contextual coherence. The injected hallucinations are constrained to be diverse across types and natural within context, ensuring broad coverage of the hallucination taxonomy. 4.1.3 Sample Quality Control. Finally, we perform systematic qual-ity control through a three-stage pipeline: (1) label normalization ,which maps non-standard hallucination type labels to the standard-ized set of 12 hallucination types through rule-based mappings (e.g., case normalization and synonym resolution); (2) text quality assess-ment , which uses an LLM-as-Judge method to score clarity, fluency, and coherence, retaining only samples above predefined thresholds; and (3) hallucination consistency verification , which checks format correctness, type validity, semantic plausibility, and contextual con-sistency of the generated hallucination labels. Only samples passing all three stages are included in the final dataset. Through this pipeline, we construct HalluScope-30K, containing nearly 30,000 samples across five task categories and eight sources, each with fine-grained hallucination diagnosis labels. Dataset sta-tistics and quality control criteria are provided in Appendix. 

## 4.2 Training of HalluScope Models 

Based on HalluScope-30K, we train two diagnosis models through a two-stage paradigm: supervised fine-tuning (Section 4.2.1) followed by reinforcement learning (Section 4.2.2). 

4.2.1 Supervised Fine-Tuning. In the supervised fine-tuning stage, we use HalluScope-30K as supervision to train the base model with LoRA [ 18 ], enabling it to generate structured diagnostic outputs that unify hallucination detection, classification, and interpretable feedback generation. This stage serves as a cold-start initializa-tion for subsequent reinforcement learning: the model learns to follow the required output format and acquires preliminary hallu-cination detection and classification abilities, reducing ineffective exploration during RL optimization [51].

5.1.2 Benchmark. We evaluate our models on the fine-grained hallucination diagnosis task. As this task involves multiple stages, we assess model performance separately at different stages. For the hallucination detection stage, we adopt the MHALO benchmark [ 4] for evaluation. MHALO is a benchmark designed for fine-grained hallucination detection and contains 2,000 samples drawn from four public subsets: RLHF-V [ 49 ], M-HalDetect [ 16 ], Geo170K [ 13 ], and MathV360K [ 36 ]. We evaluate the fine-grained hallucination detection capability of each model on every subset and compare the results with a series of representative MLLMs as well as the specialized HALODET baselines. Despite sharing source datasets, we ensure no sample overlap between our training data and the MHALO test set. For the hallucination classification stage, since there is currently no public benchmark dedicated to fine-grained hallucination classi-fication, we further construct a benchmark containing 733 samples. This benchmark is built from datasets including MMBench [ 28 ], MMStar [ 5], OCRBench [ 29 ], RealWorldQA [ 45 ], Geo170K [ 13 ], MathV360K [ 36 ], M-HalDetect [ 16 ], and RLHF-V [ 49 ], and is de-signed to evaluate model performance across multiple task cate-gories, including general perception, mathematical reasoning, OCR, spatial reasoning, and hallucination detection. The detailed compo-sition of this benchmark is provided in Appendix. 

5.1.3 Baselines. We compare our models with several strong base-lines. Since HalluScope-4B and HalluScope-8B are obtained by post-training Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct and Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct [ 2 ], re-spectively, we include these two base models as fundamental base-lines. We further compare our models against representative MLLMs, including Gemini-3-Flash, Gemini-3-Pro [ 39 ], GPT-4o [ 19 ], and GPT-5 [ 37 ]. We also include HALODET-4B and HALODET-8B [ 4]as specialized baselines for fine-grained hallucination detection. Since their model weights are not publicly available, we retrain both models following the original method and data, using the same base models as our approach. Since HALODET is designed solely for hallucination detection without type classification, it is not included in the classification benchmark evaluation. 

5.1.4 Implementation Details. We use Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct and Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct as base models. The SFT stage trains for 1 epoch, and the RL stage trains for 250 steps with 𝜆 = 0.3. Full hyperparameters, hardware configuration, and ablation on 𝜆 are provided in Appendix.

## 5.4 Ablation Study 

To validate the effectiveness of our multi-granular joint reward function, we conduct ablation experiments by training two vari-ant models: one using only the detection reward 𝑟 det (𝑦 𝑘 ) and the other using only the classification reward 𝑟 cls (𝑦 𝑘 ). As shown in Table 4, removing the classification reward leads to a decline in detection performance on the MHALO benchmark, with average 

F1 M dropping from 64.03 to 61.28 and F1 IoU from 57.57 to 55.30. This suggests that the classification objective provides complementary supervision that also benefits span-level detection. As shown in Table 5, removing the detection reward causes a more substantial degradation in classification performance on our benchmark, with average F1 Macro dropping from 51.66 to 38.17 and F1 Micro from 59.60 to 40.54. This indicates that accurate span detection is a prerequisite for effective type classification. These results confirm that jointly optimizing detection and classification through the multi-granular reward function yields better performance than optimizing either objective alone. 

## 5.5 Diagnosis-driven Feedback Experiments 

After evaluating the performance of our models, a natural question arises: what practical value does it provide? The most direct answer is that it can offer diagnostic explanations for incorrect answers, enabling self-improvement of the target model. To this end, we explore a diagnosis-driven multi-round feedback experiment on two models: Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct and LLaVA-1.5-7B. We evaluate on the 733 samples from our classification benchmark, where each target model first generates an initial response; HalluScope-8B then analyzes this response, identifies hallucinated spans along with their types, and provides explanations for the target model to regenerate its answer. This process can be iterated for multiple rounds. Detailed experimental setup is provided in Appendix. To study how diagnostic granularity affects mitigation effective-ness, we compare four feedback modes. Self-Refine [ 30 ] serves as a baseline for pure self-correction, where the target model is sim-ply informed that its previous answer was incorrect and prompted to retry without any external diagnosis. To further verify that diagnosis-driven feedback generalizes be-yond our own benchmark, we additionally evaluate on AMBER [ 40 ], an independent LLM-free hallucination benchmark containing 1,004 images. We adopt its rule-based metrics: CHAIR (object hallucina-tion rate), Cover (object coverage), Hal (sentence-level hallucination rate), and Cog (cognition-level hallucination rate), and apply four rounds of iterative feedback to the same two target models. As shown in Table 6, HalluScope feedback consistently reduces hallu-cination on both models, most notably lowering CHAIR from 7.5 to 4.2 and Hal from 33.3 to 20.5 on LLaVA-1.5-7B, while maintaining or improving object coverage. In contrast, Self-Refine may even increase hallucination (Hal rising from 54.2 to 64.4 on Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct) due to unguided regeneration. These results confirm that our fine-grained diagnostic feedback generalizes to an exter-nal benchmark and reliably improves generation quality, whereas coarser or diagnosis-free baselines provide limited or even negative effects. ## 5.6 Case Study 

Figure 4 presents four representative examples comparing HalluScope-8B with Gemini-3-Pro across different datasets and hallucination types. In the RLHF-V example, HalluScope-8B precisely identifies the numerical hallucination “six” and achieves a perfect F1 M of 1.0, while Gemini-3-Pro fails to detect any hallucination ( F1 M: 0.0). For the M-HalDetect sample containing multiple hallucination types (Object, Spatial_Relation), HalluScope-8B locates most hallucinated spans with F1 M of 0.75, whereas Gemini-3-Pro only achieves 0.47 with less precise boundaries. The RealWorldQA case demonstrates that HalluScope-8B can accurately detect subtle perceptual errors in real-world scenes ( F1 M: 0.97 vs. 0.62). The Geo170K example in-volves reasoning hallucinations (Logical_Error, Calculation_Error, Knowledge_Error) in a geometry problem, where HalluScope-8B achieves F1 M of 0.81 versus 0.57 for Gemini-3-Pro, demonstrating stronger capability in identifying logical and computational errors. These cases illustrate that HalluScope-8B consistently produces more accurate span-level detection and more precise type classifi-cation across both perceptual and reasoning hallucinations. 

## 6 CONCLUSION 

In this paper, we introduce fine-grained hallucination diagnosis, a unified task that encompasses hallucination detection, classification, and interpretable explanation generation for MLLMs. We develop an automated data generation pipeline to construct HalluScope-30K, a large-scale dataset with nearly 30,000 diagnostic annotations across eight datasets and five task categories, and design a multi-granular joint reward function to train two diagnosis models, HalluScope-4B and HalluScope-8B. Although the current pipeline relies on a closed-source model for hallucination injection and annotation, which we plan to address with open-source alternatives in future work, experiments demonstrate that our models achieve state-of-the-art performance on both the MHALO detection benchmark and our classification benchmark. Beyond detection accuracy, we find that detection and classification are mutually beneficial under joint optimization, and that increasing diagnostic granularity progres-sively improves hallucination mitigation, validating the practical value of fine-grained diagnosis. ## A Dataset Details A.1 Dataset Composition 

Table 7 summarizes the composition of HalluScope-30K. The dataset is split into a training set of 29,747 samples and a test set of 733 samples. Both sets cover all eight source datasets across five task categories, ensuring that the test set evaluates the full spectrum of hallucination types. The eight source datasets are: 

• MMBench [28 ]: A multi-discipline visual understanding bench-mark covering perception, reasoning, and knowledge across mul-tiple languages. 

• MMStar [ 5]: A curated multimodal benchmark emphasizing vision-critical questions that resist text-only shortcuts. 

• MathV360K [ 36 ]: A large-scale mathematical visual QA collec-tion spanning 24 sub-domains including geometry, algebra, and statistics. 

• Geo170K [ 13 ]: A geometry-focused dataset with diagram-based reasoning questions from Geo3K and GeoQA+. 

• OCRBench [29 ]: An OCR evaluation benchmark covering text recognition, scene text understanding, and document analysis. 

• RealWorldQA [ 45 ]: Real-world spatial understanding questions requiring reasoning about object positions, orientations, and environments. 

• M-HalDetect [ 16 ]: An image captioning and VQA dataset with COCO images, originally designed for multimodal hallucination detection. 

• RLHF-V [49 ]: A visual QA dataset with human preference anno-tations; we use the chosen answer as ground truth.

## A.2 Data Preprocessing 

Each of the source datasets listed in Table 7 undergoes a standard-ized preprocessing pipeline before entering the hallucination label generation stage. The preprocessing consists of the following steps: (1) Format unification. Raw datasets differ in schema and annotation style. We convert all samples into a unified format with fields: image, question, answer, question type (MCQ or open-ended), and source metadata. (2) Subset sampling. For large-scale datasets (MathV360K and Geo170K), we use randomly sampled subsets to maintain a balanced composition across task categories. (3) Deduplication. We construct a deduplication key from the concatenation of question, choices, and answer, and remove duplicate entries keeping the first occurrence. After preprocessing, all samples enter the hallucination label generation pipeline. The final HalluScope-30K is obtained after multi-stage quality control (Appendix C).

## B Hallucination Taxonomy 

We adopt the hallucination taxonomy proposed by MHALO [ 4], which organizes 12 fine-grained hallucination types into two high-level categories: perception-level and reasoning-level . Table 9 sum-marizes all types with their definitions. 

Perception-level hallucinations arise from errors in visual un-derstanding or information extraction, and can be directly verified by examining the image. They are organized into two sub-groups: 

• Content errors : Object , which refers to misidentifying an ob-ject’s presence, absence, or category, and OCR , which refers to misreading visible text, numbers, or symbols. 

• Attribute errors : Numerical_Attribute for wrong absolute count or measurement of a single entity, Color_Attribute for wrong color of a correctly identified object, Shape_Attribute for wrong geometric or structural form, and Spatial_Attribute for wrong position, orientation, or distance of a single object. 

Reasoning-level hallucinations originate from incorrect infer-ence, computation, or knowledge application, requiring cognitive processing beyond direct visual observation. They are organized into two sub-groups: 

• Relation errors : Numerical_Relation for wrong quantitative comparison between multiple entities, and Spatial_Relation for wrong spatial arrangement between multiple objects. Although relation errors may involve visual content, they require com-parative reasoning across multiple objects rather than direct perception of a single element. 

• Reasoning errors : Logical_Error for flawed reasoning chains or invalid inferences, Calculation_Error for mistakes in arithmetic or mathematical computation, Knowledge_Error for incorrect application of external knowledge or domain-specific facts, and

## C Quality Control Details C.1 Label Normalization 

The label normalization stage maps non-standard hallucination type labels produced by the LLM annotator to the standardized set of 12 types. The pipeline applies over 80 rule-based mappings, including case normalization (e.g., “object” → “Object”), plural-to-singular conversion (e.g., “Calculation_Errors” → “Calculation_Error”), for-mat unification for space-separated and CamelCase variants, and se-mantic synonym mapping (e.g., “Entity” → “Object”, “Factual_Error” 

→ “Knowledge_Error”). If no rule matches, fuzzy matching is at-tempted by comparing labels after stripping underscores, spaces, and hyphens. Samples containing unmappable labels (e.g., “Un-known”, “None”) are discarded entirely. 

## C.2 Text Quality Assessment 

We use LLM-as-Judge to assess the text quality of each generated sample. The judge evaluates three dimensions on a 1–5 scale: 

• Clarity (1–5): Whether the response is easy to understand, with unambiguous language and clear expression of ideas. 

• Fluency (1–5): Whether the response reads naturally, with proper grammar, word choice, and sentence flow. 

• Coherence (1–5): Whether the response maintains logical con-sistency throughout, with smooth transitions between ideas and no contradictions. A sample passes quality assessment only if it scores ≥ 4 on all three dimensions. Samples scoring below the threshold on any dimension are removed from the dataset. 

## C.3 Hallucination Consistency Verification 

After label normalization and text quality assessment, we use LLM-as-Judge to verify the consistency of the generated hallucination labels. Specifically, for each sample, the judge model is provided with the original image, question, ground-truth answer, and the hallucination-annotated response, and is asked to evaluate two aspects: (1) whether each tagged hallucination is semantically plau-sible as a realistic MLLM error, and (2) whether the surrounding text remains coherent after hallucination injection. In addition, rule-based checks are applied to ensure format correctness (proper XML tag closure, no nesting) and type validity (each type attribute belongs to the predefined 12-type set). Samples that fail any of these checks are removed from the final dataset. 

## D Implementation Details D.1 Training Hyperparameters 

All experiments are conducted on four servers, including two servers equipped with 2 NVIDIA H20Z GPUs (144 GB) and two servers equipped with 2 NVIDIA B20Z GPUs (180 GB). We use Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct and Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct as the base models. 

SFT stage. We apply LoRA fine-tuning with rank 𝑟 = 8 and alpha 

𝛼 = 16 , targeting all linear layers with the vision tower frozen. Training runs for 1 epoch with a learning rate of 5 × 10 −5, a batch size of 8, and a maximum sequence length of 4096. After SFT, the LoRA adapters are merged into the base model. 

RL stage. We apply GRPO with a group size 𝐾 = 5 and full-parameter training (no LoRA) with the vision tower unfrozen. The learning rate is 1 × 10 −6, global batch size is 32, and training runs for 250 steps. The KL coefficient is 0.01 and the reward weight 𝜆 

is set to 0.3. The maximum prompt length is 4096 and maximum response length is 2048. During training rollout, temperature is 1.0 and top-𝑝 is 1.0; during validation, temperature is 0.6 and top-𝑝 

is 0.95. We use FSDP with CPU offloading and vLLM for rollout generation. The RL framework is based on EasyR1 (built on veRL).

## G Additional Ablation Studies G.1 Reward Weight Sensitivity ( 𝜆 )

As described in the main text, the RL stage optimizes a multi-granular joint reward that combines a span-level detection reward 

𝑟 det (𝑦 𝑘 ) with a type-level classification reward 𝑟 cls (𝑦 𝑘 ). The hyper-parameter 𝜆 controls the relative importance of these two objectives. A higher 𝜆 allocates more training signal toward correct type clas-sification, while a lower 𝜆 prioritizes accurate span detection. The multi-granular joint reward is formulated as: 

𝑅 (𝑦 𝑘 ) = Iformat (( 1 − 𝜆 ) 𝑟 det (𝑦 𝑘 ) + 𝜆 𝑟 cls (𝑦 𝑘 )) (12) We train models with 𝜆 ∈ { 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7} and report results on both the MHALO benchmark and our hallucination classification benchmark in Table 10. Increasing 𝜆 allocates more training signal toward type classifi-cation. As shown in Table 10, 𝜆 = 0.7 achieves the highest MHALO detection scores with Avg F1 M=64.92 and Avg F1 IoU =59.10, likely because the classification reward 𝑟 cls also implicitly encourages accurate span detection through type-matched IoU. However, it de-grades performance on our hallucination classification benchmark to F1 M=70.70. Conversely, 𝜆 = 0.1 maximizes F1 M at 72.48 but yields the weakest MHALO detection at 61.97 and the lowest F1 Macro of 49.74. We select 𝜆 = 0.3 as the default because it achieves competi-tive MHALO detection at 64.03 while maintaining strong classifica-tion performance across all three metrics: F1 M=72.20, F1 Macro =51.66, and F1 Micro =59.60, providing the best overall balance. 

## G.2 Detection Reward Composition: Multiplicative vs. Additive 

We compare two composition modes for the detection reward: the multiplicative form 𝑟 det = 𝑟 content × 𝑟 count and an additive form 

𝑟 det = 0.5 · 𝑟 content + 0.5 · 𝑟 count . Table 11 summarizes the results. The multiplicative form outperforms the additive variant on most metrics, with a notable advantage on MHALO detection where F1 M
