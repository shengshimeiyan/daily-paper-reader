Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) in retrieval-augmented generation systems can still produce hallucinations, generating content that is un-supported or contradicted by the source texts and undermines reliability. Recent work ad-dressed this problem by training span-level hal-lucination detectors using reinforcement learn-ing (RL) and chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning. In this work, we show through error analysis that incorrect predictions by existing reasoning-based detectors are strongly associated with CoT processes that lack explicit grounding in source evidence, particularly when verification steps do not quote or verify claims against the retrieved documents. This behaviour contrasts with human verification practices in bench-marks such as RAGTruth, where evidence quo-tation is a prerequisite for determining hallu-cinated spans. Motivated by this observation, we propose an evidence-grounded RL frame-work, namely RLSeek , to explicitly enforce ac-tive evidence seeking during CoT reasoning by requiring quotation of relevant source segments at each verification step. Experiments on the RAGTruth and NewsSum dataset demonstrate consistent improvements in hallucination span detection performance, with limited additional reasoning overhead and improved robustness in out-of-domain settings. 1

hallucinations , where generated content may be un-faithful to the source, fabricate details, or produce statements that contradict the source (Niu et al., 2024). This makes hallucination detection critical for ensuring the reliability of RAG systems. Most prior work formulates hallucination de-tection as a binary classification task, predicting whether an LLM-generated output contains hallu-cinations (Huang et al., 2026; Tang et al., 2024; Chern et al., 2023). 32329 in practical settings where users need to identify which specific parts of an output are unsupported or contradicted to the source (Mishra et al., 2024; Min et al., 2023). This limitation motivates the study of fine-grained span-level hallucination de-tection, as explored in recent benchmarks such as RAGTruth (Niu et al., 2024). The hallucinated span detection task aims to localize the exact spans of generated text that are not grounded in the provided source text, enabling a more fine-grained assess-ment of output reliability. Recent methods for hallucination span detection, such as RL4HS (Su et al., 2025), train detectors to perform chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning. These approaches typically employ group relative pol-icy optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025) and optimize a span-level F1 reward that measures the overlap between predicted and ground-truth spans. To investigate the limitations of such models, we conducted an error analysis by comparing incorrect predictions with accurate ones. Our analysis reveals two critical insights :(1) There is a distinct difference in reasoning be-haviour within the model predictions. Specifically, incorrect predictions (zero span-F1) significantly rely on reasoning that lacks evidence grounding, where the model fails to quote relevant source segments. In contrast, accurate predictions (non-zero span-F1) frequently exhibit explicit evidence grounding through direct quotation. (2) This lack of grounding in models stands in sharp contrast to human verification practices in benchmarks like RAGTruth (Niu et al., 2024). In these settings, an-notators must validate each hallucinated span by explicitly identifying and quoting the most rele-vant evidence from the source. This requirement ensures that judgments are directly traceable to ver-ifiable content. The gap between model behaviours and human standards described in these two points indicates that insufficient evidence grounding is a key factor in detection errors, motivating a closer examination of how evidence is sought and used during reasoning. To address these problems, we propose RLSeek ,an RL framework designed to enforce active evidence-seeking behaviour during CoT reason-ing. As illustrated in Figure 1, the core mechanism involves instructing the model to ground each ver-ification step by actively seeking and quoting the most relevant source segments from the source text. Consequently, the model determines hallucinated spans based strictly on this quoted evidence. To optimize this behaviour, we introduce a composite reward function that integrates standard outcome-based rewards with a novel process-level penalty. This penalty specifically targets reasoning steps that fail to provide quotes or that generate text de-viating from the original source. Technically, we implement this by calculating the lexical overlap between the quoted evidence and the source text, ensuring strict adherence to the provided material. We evaluate the effectiveness of RLSeek on the RAGTruth benchmark and a proprietary dataset named NewsSum, covering diverse scenarios in-cluding question answering, news summariza-tion, and data-to-text generation. Experimental results demonstrate that explicitly encouraging evidence-grounded reasoning consistently outper-forms strong baselines, including state-of-the-art supervised and RL-based methods. Further analy-sis indicates that these gains stem from more disci-plined evidence usage achieved with minimal rea-soning overhead. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 

вҖў We conduct a systematic analysis of CoT be-haviours in hallucination span detection, revealing that incorrect predictions are strongly associated with the absence of explicit evidence grounding during reasoning. 

вҖў Motivated by these observations, we propose an evidence-grounded RL framework that explicitly encourages active evidence seeking during CoT reasoning for hallucination span detection. 

вҖў Extensive experiments on RAGTruth and NewsSum dataset demonstrate consistent perfor-mance improvements with only limited additional reasoning overhead.

Overall, these results reveal a strong correlation between span-level detection failures and the ab-sence of explicit evidence-seeking behaviour dur-ing CoT reasoning, suggesting that insufficient evi-dence grounding directly undermines the modelвҖҷs ability to localize hallucinated spans. 

3 Our Method: RLSeek 

3.1 Evidence-Seeking Reasoning 

We propose RLSeek as an evidence-seeking rea-soning framework for hallucination span detection. Our method builds on the CoT framework (Su et al., 2025) by requiring explicit evidence quotation at each verification step. Given a source text and a generated response, the model conducts step-by-step CoT verification, where each step first seeks and quotes the most relevant evidence from the source and then determines the hallucinated spans based on the quoted content. 3.2 Quotation-Faithful Measurement 

While evidence-seeking reasoning encourages quo-tation, it does not guarantee faithful use of source evidence. Since intermediate CoT steps are not ex-plicitly constrained during optimization, the model may paraphrase or summarize source content in-stead of quoting it verbatim, thereby introducing information not present in the source text (BelГ©m et al., 2025). To assess quotation faithfulness, let 

Q = {Q1, В· В· В· , Q |Q|} denote the set of evidence spans enclosed by the <quote> tag in the CoT. We assume the CoT contains L steps and denote by

Invalid quotation sets. Quotation sets that vi-olate the quotation instruction are considered un-faithful by definition. This includes cases where no evidence is quoted or where quoted spans are empty in any CoT step, indicating that the model bypasses evidence grounding or produces ill-formed quota-tions. Valid quotation sets. Valid quotation sets sat-isfy the instruction format, i.e. , each CoT step contains at least one non-empty quoted evidence span. However, format validity alone does not en-sure faithfulness, as the model may still partially quote the source or introduce content not present in the source text. To quantify quotation faithful-ness, we measure the lexical alignment between each quoted span and the source text using the longest common substring (LCS). Specifically, for each quoted span Qi, we compute the normalized overlap LCS( Qi, D )/|Qi|, where D denotes the source text. A faithful quotation should largely consist of contiguous text copied from the source. Thus, higher overlap indicates stronger grounding, while lower overlap suggests deviations from the quoted evidence. Compared to semantic similarity measures or learned methods, LCS is lightweight, model-free, and directly measures lexical overlap with the source text, making it suitable for assess-ing quotation faithfulness in CoT reasoning.

4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Dataset 

We conduct experiments on the RAGTruth bench-mark (Niu et al., 2024), which provides fine-grained span-level hallucination annotations across three representative tasks: (1) Summarization ,where a generated summary T is evaluated against the source document D; (2) Question Answering (QA) , where a generated answer T is verified us-ing the corresponding retrieved passages D; and (3) Data-to-Text , where a generated article T is grounded in structured input data D. Due to the limited availability of publicly accessible datasets with span-level hallucination annotations, we fur-ther include NewsSum , a proprietary news summa-rization dataset. Each sample consists of several source news articles paired with one generated sum-mary, where hallucinated spans in the summary are manually annotated. More dataset statistics are shown in Appendix C. 

4.2 Baselines and Evaluation Metric Baselines (1) Fine-tuned Models . We include supervised fine-tuning (SFT) models trained end-to-end with span-level hallucination annotations, without explicit reasoning. We evaluate Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct ( SFT-7B ) and Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct (SFT-14B ). We also compare with MVA (Ogasa and Arase, 2025), which detects hallucinations us-ing aggregated attention-based features. (2) Rea-soning Models . We evaluate advanced LLMs, in-cluding Qwen3 , GPT-5 , and OpenAI-o3 . We test both CoT prompting (Su et al., 2025) and 

CoT+Seek , which encourages evidence quotation before reasoning (as shown in Prompt 1). (3) RL-based Models . We compare our method against 

RL4HS (Su et al., 2025), which trains hallucination span detectors with GRPO and class-aware policy optimization (CAPO). This is the most closely re-lated baseline to our approach. The implementation details are shown in Appendix D. 

Evaluation Metric. Following prior work (Niu et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024), we evaluate halluci-nation span detection using dataset-level span-F1, and additionally report sample-level binary detec-tion metrics. Details are provided in Appendix E.

5.4 Out-of-Domain Detection 

We further evaluate the robustness of our method under out-of-domain (OOD) settings. Following a leave-one-out protocol, we train the model on two subsets of RAGTruth and evaluate it on the remaining unseen dataset. 5.5 Case Study 

We present qualitative case studies to examine evidence-quoting behaviour during reasoning, with representative examples provided in Appendix I. We observe that existing methods are often able to identify the content to be verified, but frequently fail to support their decisions with precise source quotations. In contrast, our method enforces a structured verification process consisting of three explicit steps: (1) stating the verification objec-tive, (2) quoting relevant source evidence, and (3) identifying hallucinated spans based on the quoted evidence. The case studies show that our approach produces reasoning traces that are more explicitly source-grounded for both hallucinated samples (in-cluding contradictory and fabricated spans) and non-hallucinated samples, and more closely align with human verification criteria. Notably, when hallucinated spans lack supporting evidence, the model identifies relevant source segments and cor-rectly concludes that the spans are unsupported, rather than relying on ungrounded reasoning. 

6 Related Work 

Hallucination Detection Early work formu-lates hallucination detection as a coarse-grained task, including binary classification (Tang et al., 2024) and natural language inference (NLI) (ScirГЁ et al., 2024). Later studies propose LLM-based verification pipelines that decompose text into atomic claims and verify them with external ev-idence (Iqbal et al., 2024; Min et al., 2023; Chern et al., 2023), but these pipelines are difficult to op-timize end-to-end. Another line of work leverages internal signals of the generator, such as hidden states or attention patterns (Sun et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2024), yet these methods are model-specific and cannot generalize well to unseen LLMs. More recent research focuses on fine-grained detection that localizes hallucinated spans. RAGTruth (Niu et al., 2024) introduces supervised fine-tuning based on span-level annota-tions, while Multi-View (Ogasa and Arase, 2025) aggregates attention features for token-level de-tection. RL4HS (Su et al., 2025) further applies GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) to directly optimize span-level F1, and proposes class-aware policy optimiza-tion (CAPO) to mitigate precision-recall imbalance. In this paper, we propose a framework based on GRPO that improves hallucination span detection by explicitly enhancing evidence-grounded reason-ing during optimization. 

Reasoning Technique in NLP Applications 

GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) was originally proposed to enhance mathematical reasoning in LLMs. Sub-sequent work has extended GRPO to various NLP tasks, including intent classification (Feng et al., 2025) and safety alignment (Li et al., 2025b; Rong et al., 2025). Across these applications, CoT rea-soning is widely adopted to support multi-step rea-soning and reward optimization. However, exist-ing studies show that imbalanced reward design or task difficulty can induce prediction bias and degrade CoT behaviours (Fan et al., 2025; Laidlaw et al., 2025). Recent work further suggests that in-corporating explicit, step-wise weak process-level supervision on CoT can help stabilize reasoning behaviour and improve task performance (Zheng et al., 2025). In this work, we study GRPO-based reasoning for hallucination span detection, analyze the evolution of CoT behaviours during training, and propose an evidence-seeking enhancement tai-lored to the hallucination span detection task. 

7 Conclusion 

In this work, we study hallucination span detection from a process-level perspective and reveal that de-tection errors are closely associated with degraded evidence-seeking behaviour during CoT reasoning, where verification steps lack explicit grounding in source evidence. Motivated by this observation, we propose an evidence-grounded reinforcement learn-ing framework that explicitly incentivizes active evidence quotation at each reasoning step, align-ing model verification behaviour with human an-notation practices. Experiments on RAGTruth and the NewsSum dataset demonstrate consistent im-provements in both span-level and sample-level detection performance, as well as more faithful and structured reasoning behaviour, while introducing only limited additional reasoning overhead. 32336 Limitations 

Despite the effectiveness of the proposed method, we acknowledge some limitations in our work: First, training span-level hallucination detectors relies on high-quality span-level annotations. Fu-ture work may leverage strong teacher models to automatically synthesize high-quality evidence-seeking reasoning traces and span-level super-vision, where each reasoning step is explicitly grounded in retrieved or quoted evidence. Such synthesized trajectories can be used to supervise student models via SFT, followed by RL for further optimization. Second, as recent studies explore dynamic agents that leverage multiple tools during the CoT process (Li et al., 2025a; Jin et al., 2025), extend-ing hallucination detection to such dynamic agent-based frameworks represents an interesting direc-tion for future research. 

Ethical Statements 

We use the publicly released RAGTruth dataset, which does not contain private personal data. For evaluating our LLM-as-a-judge framework, we em-ploy crowd workers to annotate model outputs with classification labels. The NewsSum dataset is inter-nally curated from publicly available news articles and does not contain private or sensitive personal information. No personal data are released in this work, thereby minimizing the risk of ethical issues.

Quo-tation diversity and relevance are crucial for di-agnosing potential reward hacking behaviours ,where the model may repeatedly quote the same source segments to obtain shortcut rewards without performing genuine evidence seeking. 

Diversity. To quantify the diversity of the quota-tion set in CoT, we adopt two complementary met-rics. Self-BLEU (Zhu et al., 2018) measures lexical overlap among generated quotations, where lower values indicate less redundancy. Distinct-n (Li et al., 2016) evaluates the proportion of unique 

n-grams in the quoted content, with higher val-ues reflecting greater lexical diversity. Together, a low Self-BLEU score and a high Distinct-n score suggest that the model actively explores diverse evidence rather than repeatedly quoting identical or highly similar source spans.
